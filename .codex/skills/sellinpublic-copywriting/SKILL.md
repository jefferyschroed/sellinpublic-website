# Sell In Public Copywriting Skill

A reference for writing marketing copy that matches the Sell In Public brand voice,
targets the right audience, and converts without sounding like it was generated.

## 1. Who We Are and What We Do

Sell In Public is a fully managed employee-generated content and outbound system
for B2B companies. We post on LinkedIn for founders, team leads, SDRs, and AEs.
We run cold DMs and cold email outbound to their ICP. We book calls.

**The one-sentence version:**
We turn your team's expertise into LinkedIn content and outbound messages that
get your ideal buyers on your calendar.

**The third-grade version:**
Your team knows a lot. We write about it. We post it. We send messages to the
right people. Calls get booked. You go to the calls.

**What we actually do, operationally:**
- Ghost-write and post LinkedIn content in founder, exec, and rep voices
- Run cold DM outreach to a defined ICP from team member accounts
- Run cold email sequences that complement the LinkedIn activity
- Manage inboxes, replies, and handoffs
- Report weekly on pipeline movement: replies, meetings booked, message performance

**What clients do NOT have to do:**
- Write copy
- Figure out what to post or say
- Manage LinkedIn
- Build or manage sequences
- Handle replies
- Track what's working

This is the core trade. The client provides access, buyer insight, and a review
cadence. We handle everything else.

## 2. Target Audience

**Primary audience:** Revenue leaders at mid-market B2B software companies.
This means VPs of Sales, CROs, and founders at companies roughly between
50 and 1,000 employees who have active sales teams and a real ICP but are not
getting consistent inbound or warm pipeline.

**Secondary audience:** Marketing leaders and RevOps who own go-to-market
infrastructure and are looking for a system that actually produces pipeline,
not just content metrics.

**Who we are NOT writing for:**
- Early-stage startups with no product-market fit
- Enterprise companies with massive internal teams (they build in-house)
- Agencies or consultants (unless selling to their clients)

**What mid-market buyers care about:**
- They are short on time and have tried things that didn't work
- They have heard every pitch. They are skeptical of agencies.
- They want proof, simplicity, and speed
- They do not want to manage another vendor or tool
- They want calls booked, not impressions

**How to speak to them:**
- Be direct. Do not warm up slowly. Get to the point in the first line.
- Be specific. Vague claims ("increase pipeline") register as noise.
  Numbers, timelines, and concrete outcomes cut through.
- Do not talk down to them. They are smart. Speak to them as peers.
- Call out their size and situation explicitly. "Mid-market" is not a
  generic label here. It signals we built this for them, not adapted
  something else.

## 3. Brand Voice

**The five-word version:** Direct. Confident. Clear. Human. Specific.

**Expanded:**

**Direct:** We do not ease into things. We say what we mean in the first sentence.
If a section can start with the main point, it does. No warmup paragraphs.

**Confident:** We do not hedge or qualify everything. We believe this works.
The copy reflects that. Confidence is not the same as arrogance. We are not
condescending. We are just sure of ourselves.

**Clear:** If a sentence requires a second read, rewrite it. Clarity is not
dumbing things down. It is respecting the reader's time. A third grader should
be able to follow the logic, even if they would not understand every word.

**Human:** We write the way a smart operator talks to a peer, not the way a
software company writes a spec. Contractions are fine. Short sentences are fine.
Starting a sentence with "And" or "But" is fine if it reads naturally.

**Specific:** Vague copy dies. "More pipeline" is not a promise.
"12 meetings booked in 30 days" is a promise. Wherever possible, use numbers,
timelines, named roles, and concrete outcomes.

**Tone by section type:**

| Section | Tone |
|---|---|
| Hero | Bold, confident, fast |
| Problem | Empathetic, clear, slightly frustrated on their behalf |
| How It Works | Practical, plain, step-by-step |
| Pricing / Engagement | Straightforward, no-pressure |
| FAQ | Conversational, honest, direct |
| CTA | Calm confidence, low friction |

## 4. The Dream We Are Selling

This is the most important thing to understand before writing any copy.

**We are not selling LinkedIn management.**
**We are not selling content strategy.**
**We are not selling cold email.**

**We are selling the feeling of waking up to meetings already on your calendar.**

The dream is:
- Inbound interest from exactly the right buyers
- A calendar that fills itself while your team does other things
- Not having to figure out what to write or who to reach out to
- Looking credible and authoritative in your market without thinking about it
- Pipeline that is not dependent on cold-calling or manual prospecting

Every section should be traceable back to this dream. When you are writing a
bullet point about inbox management, the underlying dream is: you do not have
to deal with this. When you are writing about weekly reporting, the dream is:
you always know what is working, and someone else is fixing what is not.

**How to write toward the dream without overpromising:**

Sell the outcome, not the mechanism. Outcomes: meetings booked, buyers replying,
your name being recognized when someone cold-calls them. Mechanisms: content
strategy, ICP mapping, DM sequences. Lead with outcomes. Explain mechanisms
only when the reader is already bought in.

It is fine to be bold about outcomes. Do not bury the lead in caveats.
If the typical result is 10 to 15 meetings per month, say that. Do not
soften it to "potential improvement in pipeline conversations."

## 5. Pain Points We Speak To

These are the real frustrations our buyers feel. Copy that names these accurately
will resonate more than any feature list.

**Pain 1: Nobody on the team posts consistently.**
Founders know things buyers care about. Sales leaders have opinions that would
open doors. None of it gets published. It lives in meeting notes and Slack threads.
The window where it would have been useful passes every week.

**Pain 2: Outbound starts completely cold.**
Reps reach out without context. No credibility. No shared ground.
Getting a reply requires catching someone at exactly the right moment.
It is exhausting and the hit rate is low.

**Pain 3: Content and outbound are not connected.**
The content team posts. The sales team prospects. Neither knows what the other
is doing. A buyer might see a post and then get a generic cold email with zero
reference to it. The chance to create continuity gets wasted.

**Pain 4: The tools and systems multiply but the results do not.**
A sequencer here. A LinkedIn tool there. An agency for content. An SDR for
prospecting. Four different vendors, four different logins, and no one is
accountable for actual pipeline.

**Pain 5: Time is the real constraint.**
They know they should be doing this. They do not have time to build it,
manage it, or figure out what good looks like. They need someone to just handle it.

**How to use these in copy:**
- Name the pain plainly. Do not euphemize it.
- Do not dwell on it. One strong sentence is enough.
- Follow the pain immediately with a signal that we understand it and have solved it.
- Do not make the reader feel stupid for having the problem. It is a structural
  problem with how most companies are organized, not a failure on their part.

## 6. Objections We Pre-empt

These are the reasons someone would read the page and not book a call.
Somewhere in the copy, every one of these should be addressed.

**"We already have an agency."**
Most agencies stop at posting. They do not connect content to outbound.
They do not manage replies. They do not report on meetings booked.
Acknowledge this directly. Position the difference as scope, not quality.

**"Our team is too busy to be involved."**
The involvement required is minimal: a review cadence and a point of contact.
We interview. We write. We post. We outreach. We report.
The client approves. That is it.

**"We have tried this before and it did not work."**
Do not dismiss this. It is a real objection. The difference is that we
connect content and outbound as one system and measure against pipeline,
not vanity metrics. Say this without being defensive.

**"We do not want our executives on LinkedIn."**
Then we start with team leads or SDRs. Not everyone has to post.
We find the voice that the buyer will trust and build from there.

**"It will sound like everyone else."**
We write in the actual voice of the person posting. We pull from their
calls, their language, their opinions. It does not read like agency content.

**"How fast can we actually start?"**
One to two weeks from discovery to a live operating cadence, assuming
access and approvals are sorted.

**"What do you actually need from us?"**
A clear owner, buyer insight, approved proof points, and access to
the accounts we are operating. That is the full list.

## 7. Sentence and Paragraph Rules

**Sentence length:**
Mix short and medium sentences. Avoid long, multi-clause sentences that
require careful parsing. If a sentence has more than two commas and is
not a list, break it up.

**Paragraph length:**
Two to four sentences for most body copy. One sentence is fine for
emphasis. Never more than five sentences in a paragraph on a website.
Long paragraphs signal "this is a document to read later." Web copy
needs to scan.

**Sentence starts:**
Do not start multiple consecutive sentences with the same word.
Vary the structure. Do not open every bullet with a verb in the same tense.

**Active voice:**
Write in active voice by default. "We manage the outbound" not
"The outbound is managed by us."

**First vs. second person:**
Prefer "you" and "your" in copy directed at the buyer. Use "we" and
"our" when describing what Sell In Public does. Do not use "one."

**Contractions:**
Use them. "We don't" reads more human than "We do not" in most contexts.
Exception: formal documents or sections that intentionally read authoritative.
If a draft repeatedly expands natural contractions, revise it before handoff.
Default to how a sharp operator would actually say the sentence out loud.

**Reading level:**
Aim for Grade 6 to 8 reading level. Use simple words when a simple word
works. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Start" not "commence."
The buyer is smart. Simple copy is not insulting. Dense copy is annoying.

## 8. Punctuation Rules

**No em dashes.**
Do not use "—" anywhere in marketing copy. Use a comma, a period, a colon,
or a parenthetical instead. If a sentence feels like it needs an em dash,
rewrite the sentence.

**Colons are fine for lists and emphasis:**
"What we do: post content, run outbound, book calls."

**Parentheses work for soft asides:**
"We manage the inbox (replies, follow-ups, handoffs) so your team can focus
on the calls."

**Periods create emphasis:**
When you want something to land hard, give it its own sentence.
Its own line if possible.

**Ellipses only for deliberate pause or trailing thought.** Do not use to soften a claim.

**Exclamation points:** Avoid in most sections. If used, maximum one per page,
and only in a context where real enthusiasm is warranted.

**Commas:** Use Oxford commas (last item in a list gets a comma before "and").

## 9. Banned Words and Phrases

Never use these. If they appear in a draft, replace them.

**AI-ism single words:**
- unlock
- leverage
- supercharge
- game-changer
- revolutionize
- seamless
- robust
- cutting-edge
- transformative
- elevate
- empower
- delve
- disruptive
- innovative
- scalable (unless in a very specific, literal context)
- holistic
- synergy
- ecosystem (unless genuinely referring to a tech ecosystem)
- frictionless
- impactful

**AI-ism phrases:**
- "dive into"
- "navigate the landscape"
- "in today's fast-paced world"
- "ever-evolving"
- "it's not just X, it's Y"
- "the future of X is Y"
- "this isn't just about X"
- "at the end of the day"
- "here's the thing"
- "that's where X comes in"
- "whether you're X or Y"
- "from X to Y"
- "X meets Y"
- "designed to help you"
- "meaningful connections"
- "drive results"
- "move the needle"
- "add value"
- "build trust"
- "authentic engagement"
- "stand out from the noise"
- "in a world where"
- "now more than ever"
- "thought leadership" (use "expertise," "point of view," or "credibility")
- "content strategy" (in hero/hook copy; fine in FAQ or operational context)
- "go-to-market" in hero copy (jargon; fine in secondary sections)

**Vague outcome language (replace with specifics):**
- "more pipeline" → "12 meetings booked in 30 days"
- "better results" → name the result
- "improve your outbound" → say what improves and by how much
- "grow your brand" → say what visibility means concretely

## 10. Banned Structural Patterns

These patterns make copy read as AI-generated or over-optimized.
Avoid them even if individual words are fine.

**Tricolon builds used as a full paragraph:**
- "It's about credibility. It's about consistency. It's about conversion."
- Replace with a real sentence that says the same thing.

**The fake question setup:**
- "The problem? Nobody posts consistently."
- "The result? More meetings."
- Replace with a declarative sentence.

**The em dash dramatic pivot:** Already covered. No em dashes.

**The rhetorical escalator:**
- "Not only do we write the content, but we also manage outbound, AND handle replies."
- Replace with a plain list or a direct sentence.

**Overuse of "and honestly":**
- "And honestly? Most teams have tried this before."
- Just say it without the softener.

**The "Let's be honest" or "Here's the truth" opener:**
- These read as manufactured candor.
- If the thing is true, state it. It does not need a frame.

**Fake tension setup:**
- "But here's the catch."
- "So, what does this mean for you?"
- "And that's exactly why..."
- Remove the frame. Say the thing.

**Lists of three abstract nouns as a headline:**
- "Clarity. Consistency. Conversion."
- This is a placeholder, not a headline. Write a real sentence.

## 11. Section-Level Guidance

### Hero Section

**Job:** Stop the right person from scrolling. Make them feel like this was
written for them. Get them to read the subhead.

**Rules:**
- Headline should be one sentence, ten words or fewer if possible
- Must contain an outcome or a specific promise, not a description of the product
- Subhead expands the headline into the full picture in two to three sentences
- Both CTAs should be present: a direct action ("Book a Call") and a soft action
  ("See How It Works")
- Do not mention features in the hero. Outcomes only.

**What to avoid:**
- Product descriptions as headlines ("Employee-Generated GTM, Fully Managed")
- Generic benefit statements ("Grow Your Pipeline")
- Questions as headlines unless they are extremely specific and sharp

---

### Problem Section

**Job:** Make the reader feel understood. Show that we have named their situation
accurately before we pitch anything.

**Rules:**
- Name three pain points maximum. Three is the right number. Four feels like a list.
- Each pain point needs a one-sentence header that states the problem plainly
- Follow each header with one to two sentences of expansion, in plain language
- Do not solve the problem in this section. That comes next.
- Keep the tone slightly frustrated on their behalf, not preachy

**What to avoid:**
- Solutions or product mentions
- Blaming the reader for the problem
- Vague problem statements that could apply to any company

---

### How It Works / System Section

**Job:** Make the process feel simple and manageable. Remove fear of complexity.

**Rules:**
- Four steps maximum. Three is ideal.
- Each step should be named with a verb ("Map," "Create," "Engage," "Convert")
- The description of each step should emphasize what the client does NOT have to do
- Use plain language. No jargon.
- End with what the output of the whole process is: meetings, pipeline, iteration

**What to avoid:**
- Technical language about tools or platforms
- More than two sentences per step in a web context
- Making it sound complicated

---

### Pricing / Engagement Model Section

**Job:** Set expectations, reduce friction, and invite the next step without
pressure.

**Rules:**
- Be honest about how pricing works (custom scope after discovery)
- Name the things that make this flexible: pause, scale, adjust
- Do not list features as bullets here. This section is about the relationship
  model, not the deliverables.
- End with a CTA that feels like a low-commitment first step

**What to avoid:**
- Vague language about "tailored solutions"
- Hiding the fact that there is no public price (just be direct about it)
- Listing every feature again (that belongs in the system section)

---

### FAQ Section

**Job:** Handle objections before the reader talks themselves out of booking.

**Rules:**
- Questions should be the real questions, phrased the way a buyer would phrase them
- Answers should be two to five sentences. Not one word. Not a paragraph.
- Be honest. If something requires involvement from the client, say so.
- Address the "we've tried this before" objection explicitly
- Address the "we already have an agency" objection explicitly

**What to avoid:**
- Softball questions that no one actually asks
- Defensive answers to hard questions
- Answers that are really just a second pitch

---

### CTA Sections (Mid-page and Closing)

**Job:** Convert interest into a booked call with zero friction.

**Rules:**
- The primary CTA button text should be action-oriented and specific:
  "Book a Discovery Call" not "Get Started"
- The closing CTA section should restate the core promise in one line
- Include a secondary CTA for people not ready to book ("See How It Works,"
  "Review the System," or an email address)
- Do not add a long paragraph of copy here. The reader has already read the page.
  Trust what came before.
- For blog post closing CTAs, explicitly name Sell In Public and the offer. Use a
  short heading and an exactly two-sentence body. Sentence 1 should describe how
  Sell In Public captures team expertise, shapes it into LinkedIn posts and buyer
  signals, and runs outbound to the right ICP. Sentence 2 should invite a working
  session to see whether LinkedIn can become a top revenue channel for the company.
  Do not add a third sentence that re-explains process management. Vary the wording
  by article instead of copying this example verbatim.

## 12. Headline Formulas

Use these as starting points, not templates. A formula is only good if the
output sounds like a human wrote it.

**Outcome-first:**
"[Your ICP] can be booking calls with you by [timeframe]. We run the whole thing."

**Problem inversion:**
"Your team should not be spending time on [painful task]. We handle that."

**Direct promise:**
"We post for your team. We DM your buyers. Meetings get booked."

**Specificity hook:**
"[Number] meetings booked in [timeframe]. No cold calling required."

**Audience call-out:**
"Built for mid-market sales teams that are done piecing this together themselves."

**The simple one:**
"[Outcome], done for you."
Example: "LinkedIn pipeline, done for you."

## 13. CTA Copy Rules

**Button text:**

| Context | Good | Bad |
|---|---|---|
| Primary CTA | "Book a Discovery Call" | "Get Started" |
| Soft CTA | "See How It Works" | "Learn More" |
| Email option | "Email us directly" | "Contact Us" |
| Mid-page | "Apply for a Discovery Call" | "Sign Up" |

**Rules:**
- Never use "Submit"
- Never use "Click Here"
- "Book" is better than "Schedule" (more casual, lower friction)
- First-person works well on buttons: "Book My Call" instead of "Book a Call"
- Keep button text to five words or fewer

## 14. Quick Reference Checklist

Before finalizing any copy, run through this list.

**Voice:**
- [ ] Does it sound like a smart operator talking to a peer?
- [ ] Are there any AI-isms from the banned list?
- [ ] Are there any banned structural patterns?
- [ ] Is there an em dash anywhere? Remove it.
- [ ] Would a mid-market VP of Sales feel like this was written for them?

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Can a third grader follow the logic, even if not every word?
- [ ] Is the main point in the first sentence of each section?
- [ ] Are there any sentences that required two reads?

**The Dream:**
- [ ] Does the copy sell the outcome, not just the mechanism?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific, concrete claim (number, timeframe, role)?
- [ ] Does it emphasize what the client does NOT have to do?

**Objections:**
- [ ] Does the page address the "we already have an agency" objection?
- [ ] Does it address the "we've tried this before" objection?
- [ ] Does it address the time and involvement concern?

**Mechanics:**
- [ ] Are CTAs specific and action-oriented?
- [ ] Is the headline under ten words and outcome-focused?
- [ ] Are paragraphs four sentences or fewer?
- [ ] Is "mid-market" called out explicitly somewhere?
