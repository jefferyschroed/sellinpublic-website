# Employee-Generated Content Examples From B2B Companies

Most teams asking for employee-generated content examples can't find ones worth using. The category gets blurred with employee advocacy, documentation, and product marketing until nothing feels like a useful reference point.

Good examples are simpler to identify than they seem. [claim:C001] They're public learning shaped from firsthand expertise, not employees reposting company copy. A founder explaining a category bet, an engineer breaking down a system decision, a sales leader turning a repeated buyer objection into a useful post. Those count. A company announcement shared through employee networks does not. [cite:src-008]

---

## What Makes an Example Count

The source of insight has to be close to the work. That's the clearest test.

An employee advocacy post usually starts with a company-approved asset and asks employees to help it travel. Employee-generated content starts with what the employee or team actually knows, then turns that into something useful enough to stand on its own. For a longer comparison, read the [employee-generated content vs employee advocacy](/blog/employee-generated-content-vs-employee-advocacy/) breakdown.

[claim:C006] Google Search Central's helpful content guidance asks publishers to create useful, reliable, people-first content with original value. [cite:src-007] That's a fair test for employee-led content too: does this teach something a buyer couldn't get from your competitor's website? If not, the example doesn't count.

The format is secondary. A LinkedIn post, a handbook page, a technical article, a field note from a support call, a short video explaining a decision. All of those can be employee-generated content. None of them are automatically employee-generated content based on format alone.

The lesson isn't to copy Clay's voice. It's to publish the thinking a buyer would otherwise never see.

[claim:C003] Lovable gives a different kind of example. Its public growth story and video library show how narrative, product teaching, and community-facing content can sit close together. [cite:src-002] [cite:src-003] Anton Osika's [LinkedIn posts](https://www.linkedin.com/in/antonosika/) follow the same pattern, explaining what the company is building and why, in plain language, without waiting for a press release. Not every Lovable asset is employee-generated content, but the pattern is useful: buyers see how people around the product think about what they're building.

[claim:C004] GitLab's public handbook is a durable example of company knowledge made public. [cite:src-004] It's not a LinkedIn post, and that's the point. Employee-generated content doesn't have to live only in the feed. A handbook, field note, or operating page can carry employee expertise for a long time, and it keeps working without a posting schedule.

[claim:C005] Shopify Engineering is a strong example for technical teams. Articles on sharing developer context and building resilient payment systems show how internal technical judgment becomes public education. [cite:src-005] [cite:src-006] Farhan Thawar's [LinkedIn presence](https://www.linkedin.com/in/fnthawar/) adds shorter, sharper expressions of engineering thinking alongside the longer-form technical posts. A buyer or candidate can read the work and understand how the team thinks before they ever talk to sales.

## How to Use Examples Without Copying Them

Use examples as prompts, not templates. Ask what kind of work your team is already doing that could teach the market something. Then choose the format that fits the material.

For a founder, that might be a category point of view or an explanation of a bet the team made. For sales, it might be a post about a buyer objection that keeps coming up. For customer success, it might be a pattern from onboarding calls. For engineering, it might be a technical choice with real constraints and a real outcome.

The [employee-generated content hub](/blog/employee-generated-content-infrastructure/) covers the broader operating model. This post is the example layer: what to look for, what to borrow, and what to avoid.

## How to Judge the Examples {#example-checklist}

Don't judge examples by impressions alone. Impressions show the asset traveled. They don't show whether the right buyer learned something useful.

Five questions that matter more:

- **Buyer usefulness.** Does this answer a real buyer question, objection, or decision point they'd have before talking to sales?
- **Sales reuse.** Does this give sales a better reason to follow up than a generic check-in?
- **Internal learning.** Does this turn something the team already knows into a reusable asset?
- **Search and AI visibility.** Does this give crawlers and answer engines a clear entity, source, and lesson to understand?
- **Follow-up questions.** Does this create sharper buyer questions, or just engagement?

### Copyable checklist for evaluating any example

Before calling something a strong employee-generated content example, run it through this:

```
1. Who is the source of the insight?
2. What work, decision, workflow, or lesson does it reveal?
3. Could a buyer use the idea without talking to sales?
4. Is the format right for the material: post, article, handbook page, checklist, video, or sales note?
5. What claim needs a source before publication?
6. What detail must stay private?
7. How could sales reuse the idea in a buyer conversation?
8. What follow-up question should this create?
```

If you can't answer questions one and two clearly, the example probably isn't ready yet. Start there.

## FAQ

### What are good examples of employee-generated content in B2B?

Good examples include founder essays, sales field notes, engineering breakdowns, customer-success lessons, public handbooks, and LinkedIn posts that explain a real decision or workflow. The format matters less than the source of insight. Clay, Lovable, GitLab, and Shopify Engineering are all worth studying for different reasons.

### Is a company blog post employee-generated content?

It can be, but only when the article carries real employee or team expertise. A company blog post that restates product messaging isn't the same thing. The test is whether a real person's knowledge shaped the content in a way a buyer can use.

### Are GitLab and Shopify examples employee-generated content or company content?

They're company-owned assets that expose employee or team knowledge. That makes them useful examples for B2B teams, even when the format isn't a personal LinkedIn post. The distinction between employee-generated and company-owned matters less than whether the content teaches from real expertise.

### Should every employee-generated content example become a LinkedIn post?

No. Some ideas are better as a post. Some are better as a checklist, article, handbook page, sales note, or webinar segment. Let the material decide the format.

### How do you find examples inside your own company?

Start with repeated buyer questions, sales objections, support patterns, implementation lessons, product decisions, and technical tradeoffs. Those are almost always closer to useful expertise than a blank content prompt.
