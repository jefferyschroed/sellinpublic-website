# Employee-Generated Content Examples From B2B Companies

Most example pages on this topic tell you to "share expertise" and stop there. This one names the actual public assets worth studying, links directly to them, and explains what each one shows.

[claim:C001] Good employee-generated content turns firsthand work into public learning. The format can be a LinkedIn post, a founder essay, an engineering note, a public handbook, or a team-authored article. The test is not the format. The test is whether the content teaches from real work instead of redistributing company copy. [cite:src-008]

The four companies below each make a different kind of expertise public. Clay and Lovable do it through founder LinkedIn posts. GitLab does it through a public handbook. Shopify Engineering does it through technical articles and short operating heuristics. None of them are doing the same thing, which is the point.

## What Makes An Example Count

The source of insight has to be close to the work. A founder explaining a category bet can count. A head of marketing explaining narrative strategy can count. An engineer explaining a system choice can count. A public handbook page can count when it exposes how a team actually operates.

Employee advocacy is different. Advocacy usually starts with a company-approved asset and asks employees to help it travel. Employee-generated content starts with what the employee or team knows, then turns that into something useful enough to stand on its own. For the full distinction, read the [employee-generated content vs employee advocacy](/blog/employee-generated-content-vs-employee-advocacy/) comparison.

[claim:C006] Google Search Central's helpful content guidance asks publishers to create useful, reliable, people-first content with original value. [cite:src-007] That is a fair quality bar here: if the public asset could have been written by any competitor, it is not a strong example.

What this reveals: turn product evidence into a point of view buyers can repeat. Usage data, community posts, and product tutorials work harder when one clear narrative connects them.

## GitLab: A Handbook Built From Team Knowledge

[claim:C004] GitLab's public handbook is a durable example of employee and company knowledge made public. [cite:src-004] It proves that employee-generated content doesn't have to be a personal LinkedIn post.

The handbook works because it turns operating detail into a public reference. It covers how the company communicates, hires, manages, and documents decisions. A buyer, candidate, partner, or operator can inspect the company's way of working without waiting for a sales conversation.

The LinkedIn lesson here is restraint. A handbook page can be a stronger source asset than a feed post. A team member can still distribute or explain a handbook page on LinkedIn, but the durable example is the public knowledge base itself.

What this reveals: if your team repeats the same operational explanation every month, make the source asset permanent. Then use LinkedIn to route people to it.

## Shopify Engineering: Technical Notes From Real Systems

[claim:C005] Shopify Engineering publishes technical articles that turn internal engineering context into public learning. [cite:src-005] [cite:src-006] The best examples name the system, the constraint, the choice, and the lesson.

Farhan Thawar's LinkedIn posts are a shorter counterpart. [claim:C011] One recent post gives a plain engineering heuristic: simple systems are often easier to design, faster to get right, and more reliable. [cite:src-012] On its own, that is not enough for a full examples asset. It is a feed-level signal that matches the depth of the longer Shopify Engineering articles.

Post to inspect: [Farhan Thawar on simple engineering systems](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/fnthawar_the-simple-and-elegant-systems-tend-to-be-activity-7471892411846234112-YbFc).

What this reveals: let technical experts publish both durable explanations and short operating heuristics. The article carries the depth. The LinkedIn post carries the sharp lesson.

## The Pattern Across The Examples

Each example works for a different reason. Clay's posts reveal strategic belief. Lovable's posts reframe product traction as a market shift. GitLab's handbook exposes operating detail that normally stays internal. Shopify Engineering's articles name constraints that engineers at other companies recognize from their own systems.

None of them are pure promotion. Each one gives a buyer something to think with before asking them to care about the company. That is the pattern worth studying, not the format or the posting frequency.

For sales teams, the equivalent might be a post about a buyer objection that keeps coming up. For customer success, a pattern from onboarding calls. For engineering, a technical choice with real tradeoffs. The [employee-generated content hub](/blog/employee-generated-content-infrastructure/) covers the operating model that makes this repeatable.

## FAQ

### What are good examples of employee-generated content in B2B?

Good examples include founder LinkedIn posts, sales field notes, engineering breakdowns, customer-success lessons, public handbooks, and team-authored articles that explain a real decision or workflow. Clay, Lovable, GitLab, and Shopify Engineering are useful reference points because each one makes a different kind of expertise public.

### Is a company blog post employee-generated content?

It can be, but only when the article carries real employee or team expertise. A post that restates product messaging is not the same thing. The test is whether a real person's knowledge shaped the content in a way a buyer can use.

### Are the GitLab and Shopify examples employee-generated content or company content?

They are company-owned assets that expose employee or team knowledge. That makes them useful examples even when the format is not a personal LinkedIn post.

### Should every employee-generated content example become a LinkedIn post?

No. Some ideas belong in a feed post. Others work better as a handbook page, technical article, sales note, or webinar segment. Let the material decide the format.

### How do you find examples inside your own company?

Start with repeated buyer questions, sales objections, support patterns, implementation lessons, product decisions, and technical tradeoffs. Those are usually closer to useful expertise than a blank content prompt.
