# Outline

Packet: `2026-06-19-employee-generated-content-vs-employee-advocacy`

## Search Promise

Answer how employee-generated content differs from employee advocacy, when each approach fits, how B2B teams should use LinkedIn/team-led content, and how to measure the work without reducing it to impressions.

## Answer-First Summary

Employee-generated content starts with employee expertise, observations, lessons, examples, or points of view and turns them into useful public content. Employee advocacy starts with a company-approved message or asset and asks employees to help distribute or endorse it. The two can work together, but they need different operating models, review standards, and success metrics.

Evidence note: this summary is an outline claim target. Final draft claims must map to approved citations or SME notes, not discovery-only inputs.

## Structure

1. Direct answer.
   - Define both terms in plain language.
   - State the core distinction: original employee expertise versus amplification of company-approved messaging.
   - Explain that neither model is automatically better; fit depends on the goal.
   - Claim targets: definition claim, comparison claim, POV claim.

2. Comparison table.
   - Columns: dimension, employee-generated content, employee advocacy.
   - Rows:

     - Typical author role.
     - Content examples.
     - Review and governance model.
     - Best-fit use case.
     - Primary risks.
     - Useful metrics.
   - Keep table explanatory, not promotional.

3. Why the distinction matters for B2B GTM teams.
   - Audience: founders, GTM/revenue leaders, sales leaders, and marketing leaders.
   - Explain why confusing creation with amplification leads to wrong process design.
   - Distinguish trust-building expertise from campaign distribution.
   - Claim targets: B2B thought leadership or buyer trust source; Sell In Public POV if unsourced.

4. When employee-generated content is the better fit.
   - Use when the team needs to surface field knowledge, product lessons, customer patterns, founder perspective, sales objections, or technical expertise.
   - Useful formats: LinkedIn posts, field notes, tutorials, teardown posts, customer-learning posts, founder/operator reflections.
   - Governance: brief, interview, preserve voice, source claims, review for risk.
   - Avoid: forcing every employee to post or flattening every post into brand voice.

5. When employee advocacy is the better fit.
   - Use when the team needs coordinated distribution for launches, reports, events, hiring, customer proof, or company news.
   - Useful formats: approved share copy, employee repost prompts, launch kits, event amplification, curated content libraries.
   - Governance: clear approved language, disclosure rules where relevant, simple participation expectations.
   - Avoid: presenting reposts as original expertise.

6. Decision guide.
   - Heading: Choose employee-generated content, employee advocacy, or a hybrid.
   - Include a compact decision matrix:
     - If the goal is expertise, education, objections, or category POV: choose employee-generated content.
     - If the goal is awareness, coordinated launch reach, or announcement distribution: choose employee advocacy.
     - If the goal is launch plus credible context: use a hybrid.
   - Include operational questions:
     - What insight exists only inside the team?
     - Who can credibly explain it?
     - What must be reviewed before publishing?
     - Which metric would prove useful buyer attention?

7. LinkedIn and team-led content section.
   - Explain that LinkedIn is often where B2B employee-led content shows up, but the strategy should not be reduced to posting volume.
   - Cover founders, sellers, marketers, product leaders, customer-facing teams, and subject matter experts.
   - Include examples to source during research:
     - Sales objection explained by an AE.
     - Product lesson explained by a PM or engineer.
     - Customer pattern explained by customer success.
     - Company launch translated into personal context by a founder or functional leader.
   - Link back to the hub: `/blog/employee-generated-content-infrastructure/`.

8. Measurement section.
   - Contrast advocacy metrics with employee-generated content metrics.
   - Advocacy metrics: participation, share rate, reach, clicks, event or launch traffic.
   - Employee-generated content metrics: qualified comments, profile visits from target buyers, saves, replies, sales conversation references, content ideas sourced from buyers, search and AI citation opportunities, downstream pipeline influence where traceable.
   - Note that final draft must avoid unsupported attribution or revenue promises.

9. Common mistakes.
   - Mistaking distribution for expertise.
   - Over-reviewing employee voice until it sounds corporate.
   - Measuring original insight only by reach.
   - Asking every employee to post with the same expectation.
   - Using employee advocacy tools without a point of view.
