# Outline

## Search Promise

Answer how a B2B team can turn employee expertise into LinkedIn posts without turning the output into generic ghostwritten personal-brand content.

## H1

How To Turn Employee Expertise Into Useful LinkedIn Posts

## Answer-First Summary

A useful workflow starts with one real work moment, captures the employee's judgment, turns it into one buyer question, checks risk, and preserves the employee's language. The team should not begin with a blank prompt or a company positioning line.

Claims: `C001`, `C002`

## H2 Structure

### Start With A Work Moment, Not A Post Idea

Explain raw material: objections, customer questions, product tradeoffs, implementation lessons, and internal frameworks.

Claims: `C001`

### Separate Expertise From Advocacy Copy

Define the difference between SME-led employee-generated content and company-approved advocacy copy. Do not attack advocacy. Name when each model is useful.

Claims: `C002`, `C004`

### Capture The Judgment Behind The Detail

Give interview prompts and capture rules. The goal is the employee's reasoning, not a polished essay.

Claims: `C001`, `C003`

### Shape The Post Around One Buyer Question

Use LinkedIn guidance to support clear questions, definitions, structure, and evidence-backed points of view.

Claims: `C003`

### Review For Risk Without Flattening The Voice

Explain the edit pass: accuracy, confidentiality, compliance-sensitive claims, and voice.

Claims: `C007`

### Use Public Examples Carefully

Reference Heike Young and Chris Cunningham as examples of public practitioner artifacts. Do not make performance claims.

Claims: `C005`, `C006`

### Measure Whether Expertise Became Useful

Measure buyer questions, sales reuse, relevant comments, useful DMs, and owned-content ideas rather than treating impressions as the whole scoreboard.

Claims: `C008`
