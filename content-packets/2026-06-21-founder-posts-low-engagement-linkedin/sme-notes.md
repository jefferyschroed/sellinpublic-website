## Working Diagnosis

Founder posts often fail because the post is built from the company's internal calendar rather than the buyer's current problem. Product launches, hiring updates, conference recaps, and broad founder lessons can be useful, but only when the post names a buyer stake and a point of view the founder can own.

The most useful diagnostic sequence:

1. Check who the post is written for.
2. Check whether the first line gives the right reader a reason to stop.
3. Check whether the company news connects to a buyer problem.
4. Check whether the founder adds judgment that could not come from a company Page.
5. Check whether comments are being used to extend the conversation with real expertise.
6. Check whether employees and sales reps know how to distribute the post without copying a script.
7. Check whether the founder profile gives buyers enough context.
8. Check whether cadence is steady enough to create a read pattern.
9. Check whether measurement separates empty attention from buyer relevance.

## Usable Insights

- Low engagement is a signal to inspect relevance, argument, and distribution. It is not a reason to ask the founder for higher volume by default.
- Founder-led content should carry executive judgment: why the market is changing, what buyers misunderstand, which tradeoff the company believes in, and what the team has learned from the field.
- Company Page content and founder profile content do different jobs. The Page represents the organization. The founder profile represents a person and can carry a more specific point of view.
- A good employee distribution loop is not a forced comment pod. It gives employees context, lets them add their own examples, and uses sales conversations to route the post to the right accounts.
- The best engagement metric depends on the job of the post. A founder post meant to open sales conversations should be judged by relevant comments, saves, sends, profile visits, target-account replies, and sales reuse, not likes alone.
