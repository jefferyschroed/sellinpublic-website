# Research Notes

## Summary

The query asks for a policy, not a motivational case for employee-generated content. The useful answer for a B2B revenue or marketing leader is a decision system: who can post, what requires disclosure, which customer and confidential details are off-limits, which claims need support, when legal or HR reviews a draft, and how the policy protects employee voice.

This is not legal advice. The final article should tell teams to have counsel and HR adapt the policy for their company, jurisdiction, industry, and workforce.

## Audience Context

The reader likely already wants employee-generated content. The blocker is risk: customer names, product claims, incentives, public-company disclosure, employee speech, HR issues, and platform behavior. A useful policy helps employees publish safer expertise without turning profile content into approved brand copy.

## Source Findings

- The FTC says a material connection can include employment, personal, family, or financial relationships, and those relationships should be disclosed when endorsing a brand or product if the connection is not obvious to the audience.
- FTC staff guidance says companies that pay and direct endorsers need reasonable training and monitoring programs, and can face liability for deceptive endorsements or missing disclosures.
- LinkedIn's Professional Community Policies require true identity, accurate information, non-misleading content, and disclosure when content or endorsements are shared for personal benefit.
- LinkedIn Advertising Policies require factual support for ad claims and disclosure of pertinent partnerships in advertising content.
- Intel's public social media guidelines are a useful policy example because they ask employees posting about Intel work or related industry news to identify their connection, add a personal-opinion disclaimer, avoid confidential details, and support claims with approved public information.
- Stanford's social media guidelines are useful because they separate institutional use, departmental use, and individual employee personal use. They also call out confidentiality, PII, third-party rights, account security, and protected concerted activity.
- The NLRB says employees have rights to discuss pay, benefits, and working conditions with coworkers and that social media can be protected concerted activity when tied to group action or group complaints.
- The SEC's 2013 guidance says public companies can use social media for key announcements only when investors have been told which channels to monitor, and that social media can still raise Regulation FD analysis.
- The EEOC defines unlawful harassment and says employers should communicate that harassment will not be tolerated, provide a complaint process, and take appropriate action when employees complain.
- Coca-Cola HBC's Digital Ethics Code is a useful first-party policy example for digital conduct because it names transparency, privacy, copyright, diversity, confidential information, authority to speak, and accountability for posts.

## Entity Map

- Governance: policy, review lanes, account ownership, approvals, source of truth.
- Disclosure: FTC material connection, employment relationship, incentives, sponsorship, LinkedIn personal benefit.
- Confidentiality: roadmap, internal numbers, pricing exceptions, contracts, security details, customer data, trade secrets, PII.
- Customer references: approved names, public logos, case studies, anonymous scenarios, consent.
- Claims: product claims, AI claims, competitive claims, security claims, regulated-industry claims, public-company metrics.
- Employee rights: protected concerted activity, personal views, HR complaints, workplace conduct, anti-harassment.
- Platform rules: true identity, misleading content, harassment, spam, disclosure, ads.

## Common Questions

- What should a B2B employee-generated content policy include?
- Do employees need to disclose that they work for the company?
- Can employees name customers in LinkedIn posts?
- What posts need legal review?
- How do you keep employee content from sounding like brand copy?
- Who owns employee LinkedIn posts and accounts?
- How should employees handle claims about products, competitors, or customers?

## Source Gaps

There is no universal policy that applies to every B2B company. Public companies, regulated industries, unionized workforces, and companies with customer confidentiality obligations need counsel and HR review. The article should not prescribe a single legal rule.
