# Research

Packet: `2026-06-22-review-employee-generated-content-without-killing-voice`

## Search Promise

Answer how B2B teams should review employee-generated content without turning it into generic brand copy.

The useful answer is a workflow, not a policy lecture: separate factual and compliance review from style review, tell reviewers exactly what they can change, and protect the employee's judgment unless a concrete risk requires a rewrite.

## Audience Context

The reader is a revenue, marketing, or executive leader who wants employee voices on LinkedIn but worries about:

- legal or compliance exposure,
- inaccurate product, customer, or competitor claims,
- confidential customer details,
- executives rewriting posts into company language,
- brand reviewers removing the phrasing that made the post credible.

## Source Synthesis

FTC and eCFR sources support the governance context around endorsements, material connections, clear disclosures, and advertiser responsibility for guidance and monitoring. These sources should not be stretched into a claim that every employee post is advertising.

LinkedIn's Professional Community Policies support the need to avoid false or misleading content and to include clear notice when someone endorses something in exchange for a personal benefit. This helps justify an accuracy and disclosure review step.

FINRA Regulatory Notices 10-06 and 17-18 support a narrow point for regulated financial firms: business-related social media communications can create recordkeeping, supervision, and content obligations. The article should frame this as regulated-team routing, not general legal advice for all B2B teams.

## Editorial Angle

Most approval workflows fail because every reviewer acts like a final writer. A better workflow assigns the reviewer to one of four jobs:

1. fix factual accuracy,
2. remove or route legal/compliance risk,
3. protect brand boundaries,
4. preserve voice while improving clarity.

The reviewer's comment should name the job. If the comment cannot name the risk, the edit is probably a preference.

No primary source directly proves that a specific EGC review workflow preserves voice or improves business performance. The workflow guidance is Sell In Public editorial POV, supported by official policy context and internal operating experience. The article avoids statistics, broad performance claims, and invented examples.
